Historic Context

The current waste water management issues in Lowndes County, are better understood when viewed within the context of the county’s past. Lowndes County is located in the center of Alabama’s Black Belt region, originally named for the rich, black topsoil that allowed the production of plantation crops, particularly cotton, to thrive. This agricultural industry profited through the exploitation of enslaved peoples. Many of these people’s descendants still live in the Black Belt, adding a second connotation to the term. Stark power and wealth disparities along racial lines have been a fixture in the region, with the county once being known as “Bloody Lowndes” in reference to the high frequency of white violence used to control and terrorize the black residents. By 1964 the county was 80% Black and yet not a single Black citizen was registered to vote.

A roadside sign for the Lowndes County Freedom Organization

The citizens of the county played major roles in the Civil Rights era with the creation of Lowndes County Freedom Organization (LCFO) which worked to register Black voters and introduced the Black Panther symbol which would later be adopted in Oakland, California, and throughout the nation. The county also served as a camping site for 300 protesters as they marched alongside Dr. Martin Luther King Jr on the historic march from Selma to Montgomery. This activist spirit can still be seen in the county today in Organizations like The Alabama Center for Rural Enterprise (ACRE) which are working to address the needs of hard working citizens who still face disproportionate rates of poverty, and waste water management issues that expose them to disease which have been eradicated throughout most of the U.S. for decades. When looking at Lowndes county’s past a pattern of mistreatment and neglect of the citizens appears, and the apathy towards the wastewater problems by those in power seems to be a continuation of this pattern.

The symbol of the racist Alabama Democratic party was a white rooster. To help African American population distinguish the new non-racist democratic party in Lowndes County from the racist Alabama Democratic party, they used the black panther symbol

Socio-Economic

The ethnic composition of the population of Lowndes County, AL is composed of 7,886 Black residents (74.6%), 2,578 White residents (24.4%), 50 Hispanic residents (0.47%), 43 Two+ residents (0.41%), and 8 Native residents (0.08%). Furthermore, 97.6% of residents living below the poverty line, identify as Black or African-American (Fig. 1) The largest population of residents living in poverty are females age 35-44. Furthermore, the higher-education student population collectively from Elmore, Autauga, Montgomery (Outer) & Lowndes Counties PUMA, AL is skewed away from females, with 427 male students and 102 female students(2).

Figure 1. Poverty by ethnicity in Lowndes County, AL (2016). Data derived from American Community Survey 5-year Estimate sourced from U.S. Census Bureau.

Due to economic constraints, makeshift methods of wastewater disposal, otherwise known as “straight-piping” were adopted. In 2002, 37 families from Lowndes County, Alabama were threatened with fines and jail time for not treating household wastewater properly (3) and at least 10 African American residents were formally charged with violating the Alabama State Code for operating sewage disposal systems that posed a public health risk between 1990-2002 (1). Involvement of the district court in this light highlighted the institutionalized barriers to adequate sanitation in poor, rural areas of the Alabama Black Belt (5).

Lowndes County, Alabama, given previous high hookworm burdens, degree of poverty, and use of open-sewage systems was chosen as a study site for a parasitic burden investigation in 2017. 34.5% out of 55 stool samples tested positive for Necator americanus (hookworm) (4).
